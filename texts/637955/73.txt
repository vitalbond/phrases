<div><div class="field field-type-text field-field-body">
    <div class="field-items">
            <div class="field-item odd">
                    <div><span style="line-height: 1.5;">In India, in addition to having a laws and many large civil society programs to delay early&nbsp;</span><span style="line-height: 1.5;">marriage, the government over the past fifteen years has initiated multiple national&nbsp;</span><span style="line-height: 1.5;">and state sponsored conditional cash transfer3,4 (CCT) programs with the direct or&nbsp;</span><span style="line-height: 1.5;">indirect aim of delaying marriage among girls (Sekher 2010).</span></div><div><div>&nbsp;</div><div><div>The first such program was called Apni Beti Apna Dhan (ABAD), or &ldquo;Our Daughter, Our&nbsp;<span style="line-height: 1.5;">Wealth.&rdquo; Developed by the Government of Haryana in 1994, the scheme aspired to&nbsp;</span><span style="line-height: 1.5;">enhance the value of girls. ABAD, which operated between 1994 to 1998, is one of&nbsp;</span><span style="line-height: 1.5;">the first CCTs targeting girls, implemented systematically by an Indian state. To our&nbsp;</span><span style="line-height: 1.5;">knowledge ABAD is also unique among all large-scale CCT interventions in India, in&nbsp;</span><span style="line-height: 1.5;">that beneficiaries faced a protracted 18-year period before receiving any cash transfer,&nbsp;</span><span style="line-height: 1.5;">as the conditionality was explicitly based on delayed marriage.</span></div><div>&nbsp;</div><div><div>The International Center for Research on Women (ICRW) designed a rigorous&nbsp;<span style="line-height: 1.5;">evaluation of the ABAD program and collected data from beneficiaries and comparable&nbsp;</span><span style="line-height: 1.5;">non-beneficiaries in 2012, just prior to them turning 18. A second round of data will&nbsp;</span><span style="line-height: 1.5;">be collected in 2014, after the older girls have reached 18, to measure the effect on&nbsp;</span><span style="line-height: 1.5;">delayed marriage as well as to understand the cashing out process and how the&nbsp;</span><span style="line-height: 1.5;">benefit has been used. This research brief describes the findings from the first survey&nbsp;</span><span style="line-height: 1.5;">implemented in 2012-2013. The main outcome of interest is the educational attainment&nbsp;</span><span style="line-height: 1.5;">of beneficiary girls versus comparable non-beneficiary girls.</span></div></div></div></div>        </div>
